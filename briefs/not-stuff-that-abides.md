# NOT STUFF THAT ABIDES

## a][ productions

### Title
Patterns

### Format
9:16 vertical | ~40 seconds | Visual rhythm piece with sparse VO

### Source
Norbert Wiener, *The Human Use of Human Beings* (1950)

Core passage:

> "When human atoms are knit into an organization in which they are used, not as human beings, but as cogs and levers and rods, it matters little that their raw material is flesh and blood. What is used as an element in a machine, is in fact an element in the machine."

Resolution:

> "We are not stuff that abides, but patterns that perpetuate themselves."

---

## Concept

Five shots. Each one shows the same thing: a pattern repeating, breaking, re-emerging as something else. The shots have nothing in common — different subjects, scales, color temperatures, worlds. What connects them is rhythm. Every shot breathes at the same tempo: build, hold, rupture.

The VO is Wiener's words delivered not as lecture but as incantation. Flat, measured, close-mic'd. The voice of someone who figured something out sixty years too early and is trying to keep calm about it.

The piece is structured as a countdown from machine to animal. The first shot is pure mechanism. The last shot is pure biology. The middle three are the contested territory — where it's unclear whether you're looking at a system or a living thing.

The thesis is in the editing. If you cut these images to a mechanical rhythm, they all look like machines. If you let them breathe, they all look alive. We do both. The first half is metronomic. The second half lets go.

---

## Voice

- **VO**: Male. Not narrator voice — closer to someone reading from a notebook at 2 AM. Quiet authority. No performance. Think: a scientist who accidentally wrote poetry and is embarrassed about it.
- The words are sparse. Most of the piece is image and sound design. When the voice comes in, it should feel like an intrusion — like someone broke into the footage.

## Shot Breakdown

### 1. GEARS (0:00–0:08)

Extreme close-up of brass clockwork gears turning. Oil catches the light. The teeth interlock with mathematical precision. Beautiful the way a guillotine is beautiful — the beauty is in the function. Camera slowly pushes in until the gears fill the frame edge to edge. We lose scale. Are these wristwatch gears or factory turbines? Warm amber light, shallow depth of field.

**VO** (entering at ~0:03): *"When human atoms are knit into an organization in which they are used..."*

**Audio**: Mechanical ticking. Precise. The tempo for the whole piece is set here.

**Mood**: Seductive. The machine is not the villain yet.

### 2. COMMUTERS (0:08–0:16)

Overhead shot looking straight down at a crowd of people moving through a corridor or plaza. They flow like fluid through a pipe — splitting around pillars, merging at bottlenecks. Nobody touches. Nobody looks up. The camera is far enough away that faces are illegible. They are particles. The movement has the same rhythm as the gears.

**VO** (continuing): *"...not as human beings, but as cogs and levers and rods..."*

**Audio**: The mechanical ticking continues but now it's mixed with footsteps, fabric, the dull roar of a commuter space. The ticking is underneath. You feel it more than hear it.

**Mood**: Hypnotic. Not dystopian — just what it is.

### 3. MURMURATION (0:16–0:24)

A flock of starlings at dusk, murmurating against a deep orange sky. The swarm contracts, expands, splits into two bodies, reforms. There is no leader. There is no plan. The pattern is the thing. The individual birds are invisible — what you see is the shape they make together. The shape is alive.

This is the pivot shot. The same idea as the gears and the commuters — elements in a system — but now the system is doing something no blueprint could produce. The mechanical ticking fades out. Whatever replaces it should feel organic — wind, wingbeats, breath.

**VO** (at ~0:18): *"It matters little that their raw material is flesh and blood."*

**Audio**: Ticking dissolves into rushing air. The tempo breaks for the first time.

**Mood**: Awe. The first time the piece takes a breath.

### 4. HANDS (0:24–0:32)

Close-up of a person's hands in their lap. They're fidgeting — threading fingers together, tapping a thumbnail against a knuckle, turning a ring. The movements are involuntary, arrhythmic, completely human. Small and meaningless. Not a performance. Not a signal. Just a body being a body.

The lighting shifts — warmer, softer, closer. We've gone from overhead industrial to intimate portrait distance. The hands belong to someone sitting alone. Maybe by a window. Natural light.

**VO** (at ~0:28, quiet): *"We are not stuff that abides."*

**Audio**: Near-silence. Room tone. Maybe distant traffic or rain. The absence of the ticking is louder than the ticking was.

**Mood**: Tender. Private. The opposite of surveillance.

### 5. THE FACE (0:32–0:40)

Same person. Camera has drifted up from the hands to the face. They look directly into the lens. Not a performance — more like they just noticed the camera was there. A beat of recognition. Not a smile. Not a frown. Just: I see you seeing me.

Hold this for longer than is comfortable. Social media trains us to cut away. We don't cut away.

**VO** (at ~0:34): *"But patterns that perpetuate themselves."*

The voice lands on the image of the face. The pattern that perpetuates itself is looking at you.

**Audio**: Room tone continues. One long exhale. Then silence.

### 6. BLACK + TITLE (0:40–0:44)

Cut to black. Hold for one beat of silence.

Title card: **PATTERNS** — white on black, minimal. Below: *after Norbert Wiener, "The Human Use of Human Beings" (1950)*

## Visual Strategy

The piece moves through three visual temperatures:

| Shots | Temperature | Scale | Camera |
|-------|------------|-------|--------|
| 1–2 | Cool amber / industrial | Macro → overhead | Mechanical push-in / static overhead |
| 3 | Warm dusk | Wide landscape | Static or very slow pan |
| 4–5 | Warm natural | Close-up / portrait | Slow drift upward (hands → face) |

The progression from macro-mechanical to intimate-biological is the argument. We don't state it. We move through it.

### Veo Notes

- **Shot 1 (Gears)**: Veo handles mechanical close-ups well. Reference image of clockwork to lock the aesthetic. Specify "no human elements visible."
- **Shot 2 (Commuters)**: Overhead crowd shots are strong in Veo. Key phrase: "bird's eye view, looking straight down." Avoid any individual being recognizable.
- **Shot 3 (Murmuration)**: Nature documentary territory — Veo's comfort zone. Use a reference image of starling murmuration to lock the silhouette shape. Specify "deep orange sunset sky."
- **Shot 4 (Hands)**: This is the hardest shot. Veo tends to make hands do things. We need idle hands. Prompt carefully: "hands resting in lap, small unconscious fidgeting movements, no object." May need multiple generations to get one that feels genuinely idle rather than performative.
- **Shot 5 (Face)**: Direct-to-camera gaze. Veo can do this but tends to add expression. We need neutral-to-curious. "A person looks directly into the camera with a calm, neutral expression. Not smiling. Not performing." May want to chain from Shot 4 (same person, camera drifts up) or accept discontinuity.

### Shots 4–5 Continuity

Ideally these are one continuous shot: hands in lap, slow camera drift up to face. If Veo can't do this in a single generation (likely), generate them separately and crossfade. The crossfade itself becomes meaningful — the transition from body to face, from anonymous to specific.

Alternatively: generate shot 4, extract the last frame, use it as the first frame reference for shot 5. The frame chain forces visual continuity even across separate generations.

## Audio Design

Three layers:

| Layer | Shots | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| **Mechanical rhythm** | 1–2, fading in 3 | Clockwork ticking. Sets the tempo. Should feel like it could go forever. |
| **Organic ambient** | 3–5 | Wind, air, room tone. Replaces the ticking. Should feel like the ticking stopped on purpose. |
| **VO** | Fragments across 1–5 | Sparse. The words are placed like punctuation, not narration. |

No music. The rhythm comes from the editing and the sound design. If it needs music, the concept isn't working.

---

## The Meta Layer

This is a video about the relationship between humans and machines, made by a machine at the direction of a human. The video doesn't mention this. It doesn't need to. But the fact that the final shot — the most human image in the piece — was generated by an AI model is the part that would have interested Wiener. The pattern perpetuates itself through whatever medium is available. Including this one.

Don't acknowledge this in the video. Let it sit.

## Production Notes

- **Draft-then-final workflow**: Generate all shots first with `veo-3.1-fast` (no audio). Review for composition and rhythm. Replay good seeds on `veo-3.1` for final quality.
- **Estimated cost**: 5 shots × 2 drafts × $0.80 = $8.00 drafts + 5 finals × $3.20 = $16.00 + audio ≈ **$25–30 total**
- **VO generation**: ElevenLabs. Find a voice that sounds like it's reading to itself, not to an audience. Low energy, high clarity.
- **Assembly**: Tension-aware transitions. Shots 1→2: hard cut (mechanical). 2→3: brief black insert (the machine pauses). 3→4: long crossfade (the breath). 4→5: continuous or soft crossfade. 5→black: hard cut.
