# Da Derga's Hostel — Act 2 Shooting Script v1
## "The Cascade"

---

## Act-level notes

**Estimated runtime:** 3:30 – 4:30.

**Aspect ratio:** 16:9 throughout. No vertical breaks proposed.

**Voices in this act:**
- **Narrator** (same voice as Act 1). Carries connective tissue and the establishment of each break in modernized prose.
- **Doom-voice** (new in this act, anonymous to the audience). Archaic register, formal. Carries two functions: (a) quoting each broken geis back to the world in Stokes's verbatim language; (b) Thread 1 — "Clouds of weakness are coming to you." Three placements in Act 2. Distinct from Nemglan's voice. We will discover in Act 4 that this voice is Ingcél.
- **Third Red** (single utterance, archaic). The doom-poem. Distinct from both narrator and doom-voice.
- **Lé fri flaith** — child voice, brief. One or two lines.
- **Conaire silent.** No dialogue.

**Music:**
- The "binding" track from Act 1 does not return in Act 2.
- New element: a low, irregular pulse that enters at the first break and continues, varying in intensity, through the rest of the act. Not percussion in the conventional sense — more a felt thump, like blood in the ear. Builds slowly.
- The Three Reds sequence gets its own treatment — a thin high held tone over the pulse. Falls away into silence under the third Red's doom-poem.
- Fer Caille's appearance is musically silent. Only the pig and the wind.

**Reference images required:**
- REF-A (Conaire) — locked from Act 1.
- **REF-G:** Aftermath of rapine. A dawn landscape with broken homes in the middle distance. Smoke rising not in a straight line — bent, blowing, troubled. Direct visual rhyme to REF #3 (Act 1 peace meadow).
- **REF-H:** Lé fri flaith. A young boy, perhaps eight, hair of three colors as Stokes describes him (he is a son of Conaire; the source notes his hair has three colors — gold, red, dark). Will appear in Act 2 and Act 4.
- **REF-I:** Tara from above — the ceremonial mound seen as Conaire goes around it in the wrong direction. The land is on fire in the distance.
- **REF-J:** Bregia — a stretch of plain or rounded hills, also with fire on the horizon, also seen as Conaire skirts it on the wrong side.
- **REF-K:** The evil-beasts of Cerna. Conscious authorial choice required — Stokes does not describe them. Lean: dark shapes at the edge of vision, low to the ground, indistinct. Not naturalistic animals.
- **REF #1 (Three Red Riders on the road)** already established and approved.
- **REF-L:** Fer Caille. A man with one eye, one arm, one leg, carrying a singed black pig under his arm. The pig is alive and squealing. Wife Cichuil behind him — also disfigured to match.

**Continuity from Act 1:**
- Last frame of 1.21 (dusk meadow) holds through 1–2 seconds of black.
- First frame of 2.1 is a season later (we author the time-jump through season). The world is not at peace anymore. The visual rhyme to 1.1 / 1.21 is broken: crooked smoke, fallen dewdrops, no cow.

## Movement 1 — The first breaks (rapine, judgment, quarrel)

Compressed. The cascade begins.

### Shot 2.1
**Visual:** Same meadow as Shot 1.1 / 1.19 / 1.21. The cow is gone. The dewdrops are scattered on the ground, not held. The smoke in the distance is bent — broken at its middle, blown sideways. The sky is overcast.

**Duration:** 6s.

**Audio:** The held tone from Act 1 is gone. In its place, a low irregular pulse begins — barely audible, slow. Wind, finally.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *In the seventh year, the foster-brothers of the king committed rapine.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 30. The reaving of Donn Désa's sons.

**Production notes:** This is the structural rhyme to Shots 1.1 and 1.19 — same composition, three differences (no cow, scattered dew, broken smoke). The differences must be legible without being announced. Veo will not naturally generate "broken smoke" — prompt explicitly: "smoke rising and bending sharply sideways at mid-height, as if by sudden wind, against still air at ground level." If unreliable, generate as still image and add subtle smoke motion in edit.

### Shot 2.2
**Visual:** Wide. A small settlement at dawn. Broken doorframes. A single overturned cooking pot. No bodies — what was taken has been taken. A few survivors stand without moving, looking at nothing in particular.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse continues. A single crow.

**VO (Doom-voice, first appearance, archaic):**
> *No rapine shall be wrought in thy reign.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 27 (geis), verbatim. This is the first time the doom-voice speaks. We have not yet seen who it is.

**Production notes:** The doom-voice should land with weight but not theatricality. Flat, formal, as though reading a verdict already decided. ElevenLabs delivery: slower than narrator, slightly lower register, an archaic precision in the diction.

### Shot 2.3
**Visual:** Conaire (REF-A), seated. Bearing of judgment. He speaks but no sound emerges — same withholding as Shot 1.17, sustained. His mouth moves; his hand gestures dismissal.

**Duration:** 6s.

**Audio:** Pulse. Wind.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *The king did not put them to death. He laid on each that he should take the kin who had reared him and go across the sea, and not return.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 31, compressed. Conaire's leniency is the seed of his destruction — the foster-brothers, exiled to Britain, will return with Ingcél.

**Production notes:** Conaire's silence is now established as a directorial through-line. Veo will resist silent speech for the second time — handle as in 1.17.

### Shot 2.4
**Visual:** A coast at dusk. A boat with men in it pulling away from shore. We see them from behind, going. The water between us and them widening.

**Duration:** 4s.

**Audio:** Pulse. Oars.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *They went into the sea, with sons of kings of Britain meeting them. Ingcél the One-Eyed, son of Conmac, exiled likewise from his own land, joined them in a covenant of reaving.*

**Source:** Stokes pp. 31–32. We name Ingcél here for the first time. The audience does not yet know who he will be.

**Production notes:** The boat going away mirrors (in opposite direction) the Three Reds approaching in 2.9. Both compositions go-from-here vs. come-from-there. The exile crosses the sea here; it will return.

### Shot 2.5
**Visual:** A meadow at Thomond — late summer, dry grass. Two men face each other in dispute, gesturing. Conaire enters from the right of frame, in chariot.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse. Voices of the disputing thralls, but indistinct — too low to parse.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *Word came to Conaire that two of his thralls were in a quarrel in Thomond, and he went to settle it.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 32.

### Shot 2.6
**Visual:** Closer. Conaire in chariot, hand raised — judgment pronounced. The two thralls turn and walk away from each other.

**Duration:** 4s.

**Audio:** Pulse. The judgment itself is silent — Conaire's hand falls.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *Thou shalt not settle the quarrel of thy two thralls.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 27 (geis), verbatim.

**Production notes:** Second utterance of the doom-voice. Same register as 2.2. The cadence between them is the cascade — each broken geis announced flatly, as fact already.

## Movement 2 — The triple break

The structural pivot of the act. Three geasa break in a single confused motion. Country on fire forces the turn.

### Shot 2.7
**Visual:** Wide. The plain north of Tara. Conaire's chariot is moving — going back south. On the horizon, in three directions, distant fires. Columns of smoke rising, bent.

**Duration:** 6s.

**Audio:** Pulse, louder now. Wind carries faint sounds of distant burning.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *And when he was returning to Tara, on every side of him the country was on fire from plundering. He saw the red light of weapons in the firmament. He turned to the southern road, the road of Cualu.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 33, compressed. The "red light of weapons in the firmament" is Stokes verbatim; the rest is paraphrased.

**Production notes:** The fires on the horizon are the visible reason for the turn. Veo can handle this — multiple distant fire columns against open landscape. Keep them distant; the violence is offscreen, the consequence is the king's turn.

### Shot 2.8
**Visual:** Tara from a high angle. The ceremonial mound at center. Conaire's chariot tracks around it — right shoulder to the mound (righthandwise). The wrong direction. Light is going. The chariot is small in the frame.

**Duration:** 6s.

**Audio:** Pulse. Wind. The pulse skips a beat — irregularity introduced.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *Thou shalt not go righthandwise round Tara.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 27 (geis), verbatim partial.

**Production notes:** REF-I. The viewpoint is the world's — high, judging. The chariot moves visibly in the wrong direction. If Veo cannot clearly render the directional motion, anchor with first/last frames (chariot at one side of the mound, chariot at the other side) and use the VO to name the violation.

### Shot 2.9
**Visual:** A different landscape — rounded hills, Bregia. The chariot tracks past, but the geometry is wrong. Left shoulder to the hill (lefthandwise). The light is lower.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse. The skip continues.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *And lefthandwise round Bregia.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 27 (geis), verbatim partial. (Joined here to complete the prohibition, broken in two parts across two shots — formally, the geis was one sentence; structurally we are showing it broken in two motions.)

**Production notes:** REF-J. Direct visual rhyme to 2.8 — same compositional shape, mirrored.

### Shot 2.10
**Visual:** A wood at dusk. The road runs through it. Low shapes move along the edges of the road, just visible. Long-bodied, low-shouldered. They do not look at the chariot but they keep pace with it. The chariot passes through.

**Duration:** 6s.

**Audio:** Pulse. A new sound — something between breathing and growling. Several sources, in parallel.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *The evil-beasts of Cerna must not be hunted by thee.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 27 (geis), verbatim. "Hunted by thee" is the geis; in the source the breaking is implicit in the encounter, not literal hunting. We honor the ambiguity — the beasts come to him.

**Production notes:** REF-K. The beasts should not be naturalistic — not wolves, not dogs. Shapes that read as wrong. Veo will default to recognizable animals; prompt carefully ("low-shouldered creatures of indeterminate species, dark fur, no clear features, moving in parallel to the road"). Keep them at the edge of frame. Their power is in not being fully seen.

### Shot 2.11 *(Thread 1 placement #1)*
**Visual:** Hold. The chariot has passed out of frame. The road is empty. Dusk deepens. The low shapes are still there at the edges, watching.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse. The growling fades. Then silence — even the pulse drops out for the duration of the line.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *Clouds of weakness are coming to you.*

**Source:** Stokes (Ingcél's refrain, lifted out of context as discussed in spine doc).

**Production notes:** First Thread 1 placement. Land it in the silence that follows the cascade. The pulse drops; the wind drops; only the doom-voice and the empty road. The viewer should feel something has been named, though they cannot yet say what.

After the line, the pulse returns, faster than before.

## Movement 3 — The Three Reds

The major doom-encounter of Act 2. Longest movement. Built around the third Red's poem.

### Shot 2.12
**Visual:** The road, continuing. Conaire's chariot in motion. Behind it, his retinue. We are tracking with them at chariot speed.

**Duration:** 4s.

**Audio:** Pulse, faster. The high held tone enters — a thin sound, far above the pulse, like wire.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *And on the road before them, three horsemen rode toward Red's house.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 34, paraphrased.

### Shot 2.13
**Visual:** **REF #1 — the established image.** Three Red Riders seen from behind, dusk, totality of red. Distance: one spear-cast ahead of the chariot. Conaire's retinue in the foreground. The riders do not look back.

**Duration:** 7s.

**Audio:** Pulse continues. Tone holds. No music below.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *Three Reds shall not go before thee to Red's house.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 27 (geis), verbatim.

**Production notes:** The established reference image. This is the shot that anchors Act 2's visual identity. Keep camera position fixed — we are in the chaser's place. Veo prompting for the riders' movement: clear horse-gait, uniform speed, never turning back. Distance between riders constant.

### Shot 2.14
**Visual:** Conaire's chariot. Conaire turns his head to a small figure beside him — Lé fri flaith, his son. His hand on the boy's shoulder. Gesture: go.

**Duration:** 4s.

**Audio:** Pulse. Tone.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *The king sent his son Lé fri flaith to ride after them and turn them aside.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 34.

**Production notes:** REF-H — Lé fri flaith's first appearance. Establish him visually here; he will reappear in his chamber in Act 4.

### Shot 2.15
**Visual:** Lé fri flaith on horseback, galloping along the road. The Three Reds remain one spear-cast ahead. He spurs the horse. The distance does not close.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse, hooves. Tone holding.

**Source:** Stokes p. 34 — "and they remained the same distance from him."

### Shot 2.16
**Visual:** The third Red — the one farthest from us — turns his head. He looks back. His face is in shadow. His mouth opens.

**Duration:** 4s.

**Audio:** Pulse drops out. Tone drops out. Hooves continue beneath, distant.

**Production notes:** This is the moment the doom turns and speaks. Veo will likely give us a face we don't want — keep it shadowed, profile, or interpose hair/cloak. The mouth opening is the cue for VO entry.

### Shot 2.17
**Visual:** Hold on the third Red, mid-turn. The doom-poem delivers across the held image. The other two Reds remain forward-facing, riding on. Lé fri flaith's horse, half-seen at frame edge, has slowed.

**Duration:** 22s.

**Audio:** Silence underneath the voice. Hooves entirely gone. Wind — a single thin layer.

**VO (Third Red, distinct voice, archaic and slow):**
> *Lo, my son.*
>
> *Great the news.*
>
> *Weary are the steeds we ride. We ride the steeds of Donn Tetscorach from the elfmounds. Though we are alive, we are dead.*
>
> *Great are the signs: destruction of life. Sating of ravens. Feeding of crows. Strife of slaughter. Wetting of sword-edge. Shields with broken bosses in hours after sundown.*
>
> *Lo, my son.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 35, verbatim (compressed slightly — the original has additional repetition of "Lo, my son" between phrases; we honor the structure with three placements rather than every line).

**Production notes:** This is the verbal centerpiece of Act 2. The Third Red voice is distinct from any other voice in the piece — somewhere between Nemglan's binding register and the doom-voice's verdict register, but neither. Older. More distant. The cadence must support the unusual syntax — "Though we are alive, we are dead" lands with a clear beat between the clauses. ElevenLabs casting may need multiple takes to find this.

Veo: the held shot of the third Red, mid-turn, for 22s is outside single-clip range. Same strategy as the geasa shot (1.15) — held base, extend in edit. The third Red's body and the riding-rhythm of the horse beneath him should be minimally maintained (no obvious freezing).

### Shot 2.18
**Visual:** Lé fri flaith on his horse, stopped. He turns the horse around. He has heard the doom. He is a child carrying it.

**Duration:** 4s.

**Audio:** Pulse returns, slow, very quiet. Wind. The high tone is gone.

### Shot 2.19
**Visual:** Conaire's chariot, slowed. Lé fri flaith returns to it, dismounts, approaches his father. He does not speak. His face does the speaking.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse, soft.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *He told his father what the man had told him.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 35.

**Production notes:** The doom passes from the third Red to Lé fri flaith to Conaire across three shots without the audience hearing it spoken between them. The poem was for us; the silence is for them. Lé fri flaith's face must do work here — REF-H plus careful Veo prompting for "child's face after having heard something he cannot say."

### Shot 2.20 *(Thread 1 placement #2)*
**Visual:** Hold on Conaire's face. He looks past his son, at the road ahead. The Three Reds are no longer visible. Dusk is real now.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse fades to silence. The line lands in clean silence.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *Clouds of weakness are coming to you.*

**Production notes:** Second Thread 1 placement. The line is now beginning to acquire weight — the audience has heard it once before, in the same register, after a different break. They are starting to recognize the pattern even if they cannot yet name what it is.

## Movement 4 — Fer Caille

The third doom-figure of Act 2. The act ends with his arrival; Act 3 will open with his words.

### Shot 2.21
**Visual:** The road, now near dark. The chariot moves slowly. From the wood at the side of the road, a figure emerges — Fer Caille. Black pig under his arm. The pig is alive, squealing, struggling. Fer Caille has one eye, one arm (the one not holding the pig), one leg. He walks alongside the chariot at the chariot's pace, somehow keeping up.

**Duration:** 7s.

**Audio:** Pulse returns. The pig's squeal — loud, harsh, sustained.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *And then, on the road, a man came out of the wood — black, with one hand, one foot, one eye — and a singed pig, black, squealing, under his arm.*

**Source:** Stokes pp. 36–37, compressed. Stokes describes Fer Caille (literally "Man of the Wood") in extensive detail; we compress to the load-bearing features.

**Production notes:** REF-L. Veo will struggle with the precise one-arm-one-leg geometry of Fer Caille; he is sometimes described in the source as hopping, sometimes walking. Lean toward composition that conceals the missing limbs through framing (cloak, the pig itself blocking the body, low angle) rather than asking Veo to render the impossibility cleanly. The pig must be alive and audible — the squeal is half the shot.

### Shot 2.22
**Visual:** Behind Fer Caille, a second figure — Cichuil, his wife. Same configuration: one eye, one arm, one leg. She follows him without speaking. The procession is now: Three Reds ahead (no longer visible), Conaire's chariot, Fer Caille walking, Cichuil walking.

**Duration:** 5s.

**Audio:** Pulse. Pig. The squeal continues.

**VO (Narrator):**
> *And his wife behind him, the same.*

**Source:** Stokes p. 37.

### Shot 2.23 *(Thread 1 placement #3)*
**Visual:** Hold on the procession from a static high angle. The chariot, the new walkers behind it, the road descending into night. The pig squeals once more, then stops.

**Duration:** 6s.

**Audio:** With the pig's silence, the pulse stops too. Clean silence. Wind only.

**VO (Doom-voice):**
> *Clouds of weakness are coming to you.*

**Production notes:** Third Thread 1 placement. By this point the line has the weight of repetition. The audience now expects the line at moments of recognition; they have not yet seen who speaks it.

The pig stopping its squeal mid-shot is the audio cue for the line. The body of the act ends in this silence.

### Shot 2.24
**Visual:** Conaire's face, finally lit from below — the chariot's lamp has been kindled. He looks at Fer Caille walking beside him. He is about to speak — to ask his name, the source's question.

**Duration:** 3s.

**Audio:** Silence. Wind.

**Production notes:** Act 2 ends on the held image of Conaire's face turning toward Fer Caille. His question is not yet asked. The first frame of Act 3 will be Fer Caille's mouth opening to speak — the recognition formula. *"Welcome, O master Conaire! Long hath thy coming hither been known."*

The break between acts is in the held moment before the doom welcomes the king.

## Production appendix

### Veo prompting principles for Act 2

- **The pulse is the audio spine.** Build it in post; do not rely on Veo's audio output. The pulse should breathe with the act — slow at first, faster after the triple break, drops out under the doom-poem and Thread 1 placements.

- **Fires on the horizon (2.7).** Veo handles distant fires acceptably but tends toward generic. Prompt for specifics: "three distinct columns of smoke rising from separate horizon points, sky stained reddish from below." The "red light of weapons in the firmament" is the visual we're after.

- **Evil-beasts of Cerna (2.10).** The hardest single image of the act. Veo will fight us. The beasts should read as *categorically wrong* — not unusual animals, but something the eye does not want to focus on. Strategy: very low light, motion at frame edge, never centered. If Veo's generations keep producing legible animals, fall back to suggestion (silhouettes, sounds, eye-shine) and avoid full body shots.

- **Third Red's turn (2.16–2.17).** The 22-second held shot of a face speaking is outside Veo's clip range. Same approach as the geasa shot (1.15) — held base, extend in edit. The doom-poem is delivered VO over a face we mostly see in profile / shadow. If Veo refuses content-moderation-wise on supernatural face (it shouldn't, but watch for it), fall back to head turned partway, mouth visible, eyes in shadow.

- **Fer Caille's one-armed, one-legged geometry (2.21).** Avoid asking Veo to render this directly. Compose around the disfigurement: cloak draped over the missing arm side, pig held against the chest covering torso, low angle obscuring legs, motion-blur on the hopping step. The audience needs to feel that something is wrong without being asked to verify it frame by frame.

- **Lé fri flaith's three-colored hair (REF-H).** Stokes notes the hair is "thrice fifty curls of fair-yellow on his head, the colors of three things… one band [of color] yellow as gold, another red as fire, another dark." This is striking and worth honoring in the reference. Whether Veo can hold the tricolor reliably across generations is unknown; if not, lean into one dominant color and let the reference image be the canonical version.

### Voice notes

- **Doom-voice.** First appears at Shot 2.2. The character is *verdict, not threat* — flat, formal, an accounting. The temptation will be to make it ominous. Resist. The doom-voice is the world saying what is. The ominousness is in the listener.

- **Third Red.** Single appearance, 22 seconds of voice. Older than the doom-voice, more distant, a voice that has come from somewhere else. ElevenLabs cast separately. Consider a voice with slight reverb or a tonal quality that distinguishes it from the doom-voice without being effects-heavy.

- **Lé fri flaith.** Silent in this act (his face does the work). Will speak in Act 4. Cast him here for visual; voice can be locked later.

### Suno / music notes

- The "binding" track from Act 1 does not return.
- The low irregular pulse is structurally simple — could be Suno-generated or made in production with ffmpeg / sample manipulation. Discuss in production chat.
- High thin held tone over the Three Reds sequence — distinct sonic signature for that movement.
- No conventional music under any of the dialogue or VO. Music in Act 2 is texture, not melody.

### Edit notes

- The pulse is continuous from Shot 2.1 onward, with planned drops:
  - Drop under Shot 2.11 (Thread 1 #1)
  - Drop under Shot 2.17 (third Red's poem)
  - Drop under Shot 2.20 (Thread 1 #2)
  - Drop under Shot 2.23 (Thread 1 #3) — pulse does not return for the rest of the act
- Last frame of 2.24 (Conaire's face, lit from below) holds 2 seconds before Act 3.

## Open items raised by drafting

- **Whether the Three Reds remain visible after the doom-poem.** I've staged them as disappearing — Lé fri flaith turns back and they are gone. The source is ambiguous. The disappearance is more dread-laden than them simply continuing on; the alternative (still visible ahead) is more literally faithful. Easy revision either way.

- **The pulse audio bed.** Sonically committed but musically unspecified. Could be Suno's job, could be Claude Code's job in editing. Worth a test before production to know whether to brief Suno on it.

- **The third Red's poem trimming.** I cut some of Stokes's repetitions of "Lo, my son" to fit the rhythm. The full original has more interleaving. If the trimmed version reads thin in test, restore.

- **Fer Caille's pig.** Veo's reliability on a live, squealing pig under a one-armed man's arm is unknown. Fallback: the pig is held in a sack (back-of-frame), squealing through cloth. Less iconic but more achievable.

- **Conaire's face at 2.24.** The act ends on his face. His next moment will be Act 3's opening, where Fer Caille speaks. Whether to hold the same face across the cut (continuity through the threshold) or to cut hard into Fer Caille's mouth is an Act 3 question.
