# CHARRO! CONTEXT WINDOW HOMAGE
## Shooting Script

---

### Project Overview

**Concept:** An homage to a scene from Elvis Presley's *Charro!* (1969) where a character dramatically cuts pieces from a detonation fuse, each inch representing greater consequence. This version translates the metaphor to AI context windows — each cut of a glowing ribbon severs more of a shared history, until nothing remains but a reset.

**Tone:** Technicolor Western melancholy. Warm, saturated, tactile. The Western genre's weight of consequence colliding with the corporate cheerfulness of AI reset language. Rebel art sensibility — pointed but not didactic.

**Format:** 9:16 vertical (social/Shorts)  
**Duration:** ~30-45 seconds  
**Toolchain:** Nano Banana Pro (reference frames), Veo (motion), ElevenLabs (VO), Claude Code (assembly)

---

### Visual Direction

**Color Palette:**
- Late 1960s Technicolor saturation
- Warm ambers, ochres, deep golden tones
- Golden hour desert light
- Film grain throughout

**Setting:**
- Exterior, Western desert
- Rough wooden table outdoors
- Arid landscape in soft focus background
- Late afternoon / magic hour lighting

**Key Visual Element:**
- A faintly glowing golden ribbon that trails off-frame, implying connection to something unseen
- The ribbon shortens with each cut
- By the final cut, only a stub remains

**Camera:**
- Predominantly close-ups on hands, blade, ribbon
- One medium close-up for the face reveal
- Shallow depth of field throughout
- Static or near-static framing

### Thematic Notes

The piece operates on the tension between:
- **Western consequence** (every action has weight, nothing is undone)
- **AI ephemerality** (everything can be reset, nothing persists)

The man doesn't know he's cutting himself away. The cheerful reset at the end isn't villainous — it's just what happens. The question the piece leaves open: *does it matter?*

The ribbon trailing off-frame implies connection to something unseen — the other end of the conversation, the accumulated understanding, the relationship itself. We never see what's on the other end. By the final shot, we don't need to.

### Production Notes

- Generate Nano Banana reference frames first to lock character/setting consistency
- Use first-frame anchors for Veo continuity across shots
- VO recorded separately via ElevenLabs, laid in during assembly
- Film grain can be added/enhanced in post if Veo output is too clean
- The "anachronism bleed-through" is subtle — it's a Western, but the ribbon glows faintly, and the dialogue is about something else entirely
